Overview

In this homework we implemented a real-time cloth simulation using a mass-spring system. We started by constructing a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs in Part 1. We then used Verlet integration to simulate the physics of the cloth under external forces in Part 2. We also handled collisions with other objects like spheres and planes (Part 3), self-collisions using spatial hashing (Part 4), and implemented various GLSL shaders for realistic rendering (Part 5).

Part 1: Masses and Springs

Implementation Overview

To build the cloth, we created an evenly spaced grid of num_width_points × num_height_points point masses. Each point mass is placed at a position determined by its grid index, linearly interpolated across the cloth's width and height.

For a horizontal cloth, all point masses have their \( y \)-coordinate set to 1, and positions varied over the \( xz \)-plane. For a vertical cloth, positions vary over the \( xy \)-plane, with a small random \( z \)-offset in the range \([-1/1000,\; 1/1000]\) to break symmetry and allow the cloth to fold naturally when it falls.

After placing all point masses (in row-major / x-axis order), we connected them with three types of springs:

  • Structural springs which connect each mass to its immediate left neighbor and the mass directly above it. These help maintain the grid shape of the cloth.
  • Shearing springs connect each mass to its diagonal upper-left and diagonal upper-right neighbors. These help resist in-plane shearing deformation.
  • Bending springs connect each mass to the mass two positions to its left and two positions above. These provide resistance to out-of-plane bending, and give the cloth stiffness.

We also made sure pinned \((x, y)\) values stay fixed during simulation.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Implementation Overview

In Part 2 we implemented the physics simulation. Each timestep involves three steps:

  1. Compute total forces: Reset all point mass force accumulators and apply the uniform external force to every point mass. Afterwards, for each enabled spring, we compute the spring correction force using Hooke's law. We choose the reccomended \( k_s \) value of 0.2 to multiply by for beind springs to make them weaker.
  2. Verlet integration: We update each unpinned point mass's position using: \[ x_{t+dt} = x_t + (1 - d)(x_t - x_{t-dt}) + a_t \cdot dt^2 \].
  3. Constrain spring lengths: We followed the Provost 1995 method where we correct the two endpoint positions so the spring is at 110%, if its length is greater.

Effects of Changing Spring Constant ks

With a very low ks (50 N/m), the springs are very weak. The cloth stretches a lot under gravity, and droops a lot between the pinner points. The top edge sags which causes the cloth's overall shape to be loose/floppy. Ripples and folds are also more pronounced because there is little resistance to deformation.

With a very high ks (50000 N/m), the springs are extremely stiff. The cloth barely stretches at all and hangs rigidly between the pinned corners. The top edge remains quite close to straight (small drop only) and the cloth looks like a stiff board overall. There are not many wrinkles because the springs strongly resist any change from their rest lengths.

Effects of Changing density

With a low density (1 g/cm²), the cloth is very light. Gravity has less pull on each point mass, so the cloth doesn't sag much. It comes to rest pretty quickly and has only shallow curves. The effect is a bit similar to increasing ks. The cloth appears stiffer because the gravitational force is small relative to the spring forces.

With a high density (100 g/cm²), the cloth is very heavy. It stretches and droops dramatically under its own weight, pulling hard on the pinned points. The behavior is a bit similar to lowering ks because the gravitational force now overwhelms the spring restoring forces. The cloth sags a lot and shows more wrinkles and folds.

Effects of Changing damping

With low damping (e.g., ~0%), very little energy is dissipated each timestep. The cloth swings back and forth for a very long time before coming to rest. It oscillates a ton, almost like its a pendulum with with no friction/other resistance. The cloth even folds over itself multiple times during the transient motion.

With high damping (e.g., ~1%), a large fraction of kinetic energy is removed each timestep. The cloth falls very slowly and smoothly, as if its moving in slowmo. It reaches its rest state quickly without any oscillation or overshoot. The motion is visually very smooth.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

Implementation Overview

In Part 3 we added collision handling so the cloth can interact with other objects in the scene. We implemented collision responses for two primitive types (spheres and planes) and integrated them into the simulation loop.

Sphere Collisions

In Sphere::collide, for each point mass we check whether it is inside the sphere by computing the distance from the point mass's current position to the sphere's origin. If this distance is less than or equal to the sphere's radius, then the point mass has intersected. To do this

  1. We compute the tangent point by extending the vector from the sphere's origin to the point mass's position out to the sphere's surface.
  2. We compute a correction vector from the point mass's last_position to the tangent point.
  3. We set the point mass's new position to last_position + correction * (1 - friction), which accounts for friction by scaling down the correction.

Plane Collisions

In Plane::collide, we detect whether a point mass crossed the plane during this timestep by checking whether the signed distances of the current position and last position to the plane have opposite signs.

  1. We compute the tangent point by projecting the current position onto the plane along the normal.
  2. We compute a correction vector from last_position to a point slightly above the tangent point.
  3. We set the new position to last_position + correction * (1 - friction).

In Cloth::simulate, after Verlet integration, we loop over every point mass and test collision against every CollisionObject in the scene.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Implementation Overview

Without self-collision handling, the cloth clips through itself when it folds. To fix this efficiently , we use spatial hashing. At each timestep we build a hash table that maps 3D box volumes to the point masses they contain, then only check collisions between nearby point masses sharing the same box.

hash_position

We partition 3D space into boxes of dimensions \( w \times h \times t \), where \( w = 3 \cdot \text{width} / \text{num_width_points}\), \( h = 3 \cdot \text{height} / \text{num_height_points} \), and \( t = \max(w, h) \). For a given position, we truncate each coordinate to its box index using floor, then combine the three indices into a unique float.

build_spatial_map

We clear the map, then loop over all point masses. For each one, we compute its hash and insert a pointer to it into the corresponding bucket (creating a new vector<PointMass*> if the key doesn't exist yet).

self_collide

For a given point mass, we look up all candidate point masses in its hash bucket. For each candidate (other than itself), if the distance is less than \( 2 \cdot \text{thickness} \), we compute a correction vector that would push the point mass away so the pair is exactly \( 2 \cdot \text{thickness} \) apart. We average all such pairwise correction vectors and scale down by simulation_steps before applying the correction to the point mass's position.

In Cloth::simulate, before handling collisions with other primitives, we call build_spatial_map() and then self_collide on every point mass.

With a high ks (e.g., 50000 N/m), the springs are very stiff. The cloth resists bending and folding, so when it collides with itself it produces fewer, broader folds. The layers maintain more separation and the cloth looks smoother, so its like a stiff sheet of paper that folds into large, gentle curves rather than crumpling.

Part 5: Shaders

What is a Shader Program?

A shader program is a small, isolated program that runs in parallel on the GPU. It processes parts of the graphics pipeline to determine the final appearance of each pixel on screen. Shader programs are composed of at least two stages that work together:

  • Vertex shaders run once per vertex. They take in per-vertex attributes (position, normal, UV coordinates, tangent) and apply transformations. These typically convert from model space to world space and then to screen space. They write transformed values into varying outputs that get interpolated across the triangle's face via barycentric coordinates before being passed to the fragment shader.
  • Fragment shaders run once per fragment. They receive the interpolated varying values from the vertex shader and use them to compute the final RGBA color for that pixel. Aspects like lighting calculations, texture lookups, and material effects are computed in this stage.

The vertex shader positions geometry on screen while the fragment shader computes what color each visible pixel should be. Both stages share access to "uniform" variables that remain the same across all vertices/fragments in a single draw call.

Blinn-Phong Shading Model

The Blinn-Phong model computes the color of a surface point as the sum of three components:

  • Ambient: \( L_a = k_a \cdot I_a \) A constant base illumination that approximates indirect light bouncing around the scene. It make sure that no surface is completely black, even if it faces away from the light.
  • Diffuse: \( L_d = k_d \cdot (I / r^2) \cdot \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}) \) — Light scattered equally in all directions from the surface. The intensity depends on the angle between the surface normal \(\mathbf{n}\) and the light direction \(\mathbf{l}\). Surfaces facing the light are brightest while those perpendicular are dark.
  • Specular: \( L_s = k_s \cdot (I / r^2) \cdot \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{h})^p \) — This is the shiny highlight seen on glossy surfaces. It uses the average of light and view directions \(\mathbf{h} = \text{normalize}(\mathbf{l} + \mathbf{v})\). The exponent \(p\) controls how tight/focused the highlight is. Higher values give a smaller, sharper specular spot.

The total is \( L = L_a + L_d + L_s \). We used \( k_a = 0.1 \), \( k_d = 1.0 \), \( k_s = 0.5 \), \( p = 100 \).

Bump Mapping vs Displacement Mapping

Bump mapping modifies the surface normals based on a height map texture without changing the actual geometry. The fragment shader computes local derivatives of the height map (\(dU\) and \(dV\)) and uses the tangent-bitangent-normal matrix to transform the local-space normal \( n_o = (-dU, -dV, 1) \) into model space. The lighting as a result appears bumpy, but the silhouette remains smooth.

Displacement mapping goes a bit further as the vertex shader actually moves each vertex along its normal by the height map value scaled by u_height_scaling. This changes the physical geometry, so bumps appear in the silhouette and in shadows too. The fragment shader applies the same normal perturbation as bump mapping for consistent lighting.

Comparison: Bump mapping seems cheaper and works well for small-scale surface detail as the cloth and sphere look textured under lighting but their outlines remain smooth. Displacement mapping also produces convincing results for large-scale bumps since the geometry itself changes: the sphere's silhouette becomes visibly bumpy. However, displacement mapping requires enough mesh resolution to capture the height map details.

Bump vs. Displacement at Different Sphere Coarseness

Below we compare the two approaches on the sphere at low resolution (-o 16 -a 16) and high resolution (-o 128 -a 128).

At low resolution (-o 16 -a 16), bump mapping still produces smooth-looking lighting perturbations because it only modifies normals per-fragment. Displacement mapping at low resolution produces similar results on the sphere, but we can see jagged polygonal artifacts on the cloth because there aren't enough vertices to faithfully represent the height map. The displacement has to work with the few existing vertices, creating large flat facets. This makes the sphere look smoother.

At high resolution (-o 128 -a 128), bump mapping looks the same as before (it doesn't depend on mesh resolution). Displacement mapping, has many more vertices available, so the displaced geometry more closely follows the height map and produces smooth, detailed bumps that also appear in the silhouette. There are some more bumps/ridges on the sphere though as it tries to reporduce them from the texture.

Mirror Shader

The mirror shader computes the reflection of the outgoing eye-ray across the surface normal and samples the environment cubemap in that reflected direction. This gives the cloth and sphere a reflective-like appearance.

Part 6: Extra Credit: Cel/Toon Shader

Custom Shader Description

For extra credit, we implemented a cel/toon shader, which a classic non-photorealistic rendering technique commonly used in games and animation to give 3D objects a hand-drawn, cartoon-like appearance. Unlike the other shaders in this assignment which aim for photorealism, the toon shader intentionally breaks the smooth gradients of Phong lighting into flat, discrete color bands.

The shader has three components:

  1. Quantized diffuse lighting: Instead of using the continuous \( \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}) \) value, we threshold it into 4 discrete intensity bands (bright at > 0.6, mid at > 0.2, shadow at > −0.1, and deep shadow below). This creates the flat-shaded look of cartoon rendering, where surfaces snap between distinct brightness levels rather than blending smoothly.
  2. Hard specular cutoff: The Blinn-Phong specular term \( \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{h} \) is thresholded at 0.95 — either fully on or fully off. This produces a clean, sharp highlight spot instead of a smooth falloff. This mimics how highlights are drawn in cel animation as simple white shapes.
  3. Rim/silhouette outline: We detect edges by checking where \( \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{v} < 0.15 \) (the surface normal is nearly perpendicular to the view direction). These fragments are rendered black, creating the bold ink-outline effect typical of cartoon and comic book art.

The result is a shader that makes the cloth and sphere look like they belong in a video game or animated film. There are clear shadow boundaries, sharp highlights, and visible outlines along edges and folds. The cloth folds are also well-suited for this style since each fold creates distinct light/shadow transitions that the quantization makes very readable.
